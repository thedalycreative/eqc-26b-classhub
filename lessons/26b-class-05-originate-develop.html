§ 02 — Subject 2 unit

BSBCRT512 — Originate & Develop Concepts.

Plain English version. The unit asks you to take a real issue, generate ideas to address it, evaluate those ideas honestly, present one to stakeholders, and refine it based on feedback. Four moves, in order.

1

Scope the issue

Workplace, community, or business. Specific enough to research, big enough to matter. No "make an app" energy.

Maps to PC 1.1–1.4
2

Generate & evaluate

Multiple ideas, brainstormed wide. Cost, capability, timeline, fit. Cut what doesn't survive scrutiny.

Maps to PC 2.1–2.3
3

Present to stakeholders

One concept, in a format that fits the audience. Slides, doc, prototype — whatever lands the idea cleanly.

Maps to PC 2.4 + 3.1–3.2
4

Refine on feedback

Document what changed. Answer questions with substance. Revise until it's actually implementable.

Maps to PC 3.3–3.4
Trainer-only · presenter notes

Anchor the unit in something real

Use a worked example: walk through a past student concept (anonymised) start-to-finish so they see what each step looks like in practice. The "evaluate honestly" step is where students usually skip — call it out.

Watch for: students fixating on a "cool idea" before they've nailed the issue. Push them back to step 1 every time.

Originate and Develop Concepts — the in-class activity.

You'll be given a short business case — the kind of issue an organisation might actually face. From there, the activity is yours: identify the issue, develop two solution paths, pick one, present it, and refine it based on feedback.

Bring a laptop and an open mind. No pre-reading required.

Trainer-only · presenter notes

How to walk this section

Open expand-all. Step through Part 1 first — emphasise that questions 1–8 are about solution one, then questions 9–11 are the evaluation. Same shape repeats for solution two (12–21).

Part 2 is the pitch. 5 minutes, last 2 minutes are Q&A. Remind them this is a soft skills assessment as much as a content one.

Parts 3 & 4 are short but high-leverage. Feedback documentation + reflection. Easy marks if they actually take notes during the pitch.

§ 04 — Concept lens

The four filters of a good concept.

Every concept that survives this unit passes through these four filters. If it fails one, you fix it or you bin it. Use this as your gut-check tomorrow when you're choosing what to develop.

Filter 01 · Reality check

Viable

  • Can this actually be built with available time, money, skills?
  • Does it match the constraints of the brief?
  • Are there legal, ethical, or operational blockers?
  • Maps to: PC 1.4 — assess factors affecting viability
Filter 02 · Differentiator

Original

  • How does it differ from existing solutions?
  • What gap does it fill that competitors miss?
  • Have you compared it against best-practice examples?
  • Maps to: PC 2.3 — compare with best practice
Filter 03 · Communicable

Presentable

  • Can a stakeholder understand it in 60 seconds?
  • Is the format right for the audience (slides, doc, demo)?
  • Does it land the value, not just the features?
  • Maps to: PC 2.4 — select & present a solution
Filter 04 · Adaptable

Refinable

  • Can it absorb feedback without falling apart?
  • Have you actually sought stakeholder input?
  • Did you document what changed and why?
  • Maps to: PC 3.1–3.4 — seek feedback & refine
Anti-pattern · Avoid

The "cool idea" trap

  • Concept is exciting but solves no real issue
  • No comparable products researched
  • Demo-driven, not stakeholder-driven
  • Symptoms: starts with "wouldn't it be cool if…"
Anti-pattern · Avoid

The "first idea" trap

  • Locked in before brainstorming alternatives
  • No evaluation against viability factors
  • Refinement = polish, not rethink
  • Symptoms: skipped step 2, jumped to step 4
