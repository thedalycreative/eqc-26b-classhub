1
Test yourself — don't re-read
Dunlosky 2013 · Hattie & Donoghue 2021 (242 studies)

Do this: shut the book and scribble down everything you remember before you look again.

"Re-reading your notes feels productive — but it's a trap. The single best thing you can do is shut the book and try to remember it. It's called retrieval practice, and across hundreds of studies it beats re-reading every single time. Struggling to recall it is the bit that makes it stick."

Space it out — cramming leaks
Distributed practice · Hattie & Donoghue 2021

Do this: three 20-minute sessions across the week beat one 60-minute panic.

"Cramming the night before is like filling a bucket with a hole in it — most of it leaks out by morning. Spread the same study time across a few days and you'll remember way more, for way longer. Same effort, better timing. Your future self says thanks."

Mix your topics (interleaving)
Rohrer & Taylor 2007 · +30–50% on new problems

Do this: jumble different problem types together instead of doing 20 of the same in a row.

"Here's a weird one. Instead of drilling one topic till you're sick of it, mix a few together. It feels harder — and that's the point. Students who jumbled their practice scored thirty to fifty per cent better on new problems. Mixing forces your brain to pick the right tool, not just autopilot."

4
Write it by hand
van der Meer et al., Frontiers in Psychology 2023 (EEG)

Do this: take key notes with a pen — save the laptop for long essays.

"Typing is faster, sure. But brain scans show writing by hand lights up way more of your brain — the bits tied to memory and learning. When you type, you transcribe. When you write, you actually process it. So for the notes that matter, grab a pen."

Teach it to steal it (Protégé Effect)
The Protégé Effect · learning-by-teaching research

Do this: explain today's topic to a mate (or your dog) like they've never heard it.

"Want to know if you really get something? Try teaching it. The second you explain it to someone else, your brain finds every gap you didn't know you had. It's called the protégé effect — we learn best when we prep to teach. So turn to the person next to you and explain it."

Review right before sleep
Sleep & memory consolidation research

Do this: do a quick recap of the hardest bit just before bed — then actually sleep.

"Your brain doesn't switch off at night — it files everything you learned that day into long-term storage while you sleep. So a quick review right before bed gives your memory a head start. And pulling an all-nighter? That's deleting the very thing that saves it."

Quizzing actually calms nerves
Agarwal et al. 2014

Do this: low-stakes self-quizzing all term — not one terrifying review at the end.

"Sounds backwards, but testing yourself regularly actually lowers exam stress. When students did low-stakes quizzes through the term, their exam anxiety went down and confidence went up. Familiar beats scary. The more you practise being tested, the less the real one rattles you."

The Zeigarnik effect

Do this: pause a task half-done — your brain keeps chewing on it in the background.

If it feels easy, it's not working
Desirable difficulties · Bjork

Do this: pick the study method that feels harder — that's the one building memory.

"Big myth: if studying feels smooth, it's working. Usually the opposite. Highlighting feels easy and does almost nothing. The methods that feel like a struggle — recalling, mixing, self-testing — are the ones actually building memory. Researchers call it desirable difficulty. Lean into the hard."

"Final one. Take whatever you're learning and explain it like you're talking to a five-year-old — no jargon allowed. The moment you reach for a fancy word to hide behind, you've found the bit you don't actually understand. Simple language is the ultimate test of real knowledge."
