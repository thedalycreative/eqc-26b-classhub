wireframe — a low-fidelity visual plan that answers what goes where. not what colour. not what font. not what the hero image looks like. just: here's the shape of the thing. grey boxes for images. placeholder text. no styling. the point is structure, hierarchy, and flow — agreed before the expensive work starts. it's the blueprint, not the building. you don't paint a house before the walls are up. same energy.

T
tim_daly trainer Today at 10:10 AM
why plan? three reasons, ranked by how much they'll save your sanity ↓
REASON 01
Catch the gaps
You'll find missing pages, missing copy, missing flows — while it costs minutes to fix, not days. Better to discover "wait, where does the contact form live?" on paper than three days into the build.
REASON 02
Align with the client
Show structure before style. Disagreements about layout are easier to settle on a sketch than on built code. "Can we move that?" is fine on a wireframe. It's a refactor in production.
REASON 03
Protect your time
The number of times a wireframe has saved me from rebuilding a whole page from scratch — I've stopped counting. The 10 minutes spent planning are the 2 hours you didn't lose to a rebuild.
10 min planning ≈ 1 hour building ≈ a weekend of rebuilding
T
tim_daly trainer Today at 10:14 AM
there's a whole spectrum from napkin sketch to built and live. each step costs more to change than the one before. catch problems on the left. the right gets expensive fast.
THE FIDELITY SPECTRUM
cost to change increases →
Sketch
5 min · paper
Wireframe
30 min · Figma
Mockup
2–4 hrs · Figma
Prototype
½ day · clickable
Built
days+ · code
discovering a problem at "built" costs days. discovering it at "sketch" costs nothing.
T
tim_daly trainer Today at 10:18 AM
this is the order. memorise it, tattoo it, whatever. it works for a 1-page portfolio and a 50-page e-commerce site. skip steps and you'll pay for it in rebuilds.
THE PROCESS
Same order. Every project.
  1. Discover — who's the audience? what's the goal? what does "done" mean?
  2. Sitemap — what pages exist and how do they connect?
  3. User flows — what's the path through a task ("buy a thing", "book a class")?
  4. Wireframes — page-by-page structure, no style.
  5. Content — real words. lorem ipsum is a cope.
  6. Mockups — apply the visual design.
  7. Prototype — click through to test the flows.
  8. Build — code it. finally.
  9. Test & ship — watch someone actually use it.
do them in order. all of them. yes, even the boring ones.

T
tim_daly trainer Today at 11:02 AM
before we talk about which AI to use, four things about ChatGPT and Copilot that didn't make it onto their landing pages ↓
APRIL 2023 · SAMSUNG
Samsung banned ChatGPT company-wide.
In 20 days, Samsung engineers leaked source code into ChatGPT three times. Once it's in OpenAI's servers, you can't pull it back. The lesson isn't "Samsung engineers bad" — it's "this happens fast and the data is gone."
DECEMBER 2024 · ITALY
€15M fine for training on personal data.
Italy's data protection authority hit OpenAI with the first GDPR fine ever issued against a generative AI company — for using personal data to train ChatGPT without legal basis. (Overturned on appeal March 2026, but the case still reshaped EU law and policy.)
ONGOING · GITHUB COPILOT
Class action over open source code.
Copilot was trained on public GitHub repos — much of it open source code with licenses Microsoft didn't honour. Copilot has been caught suggesting near-verbatim copies of training code, including comments and variable names. Imagine LimeWire 2003 but for git repos.
RIGHT NOW · DEFAULT SETTINGS
Your conversations train the model.
On free / personal tiers, by default, every prompt feeds back into training. Including anything you paste in. Read the terms before you paste a client brief, a contract, or someone's medical record (real things people have done).
none of this means "never use them". it means "know what you're using."

prompt craft isn't a special skill. it's just: give the AI what a thoughtful human would need to do the job. — set the role — give the context (audience, goal, tone, constraints) — show an example if you can — specify the output format — iterate. the first answer is a draft. tell it what to fix.
write me a homepage for a coffee shop // ↑ this is a wish, not a prompt
You are a copywriter for small Perth hospitality businesses. Write a homepage for Pulse Coffee, a Mt Lawley specialty cafe. Audience: locals 25–40, want good coffee without pretension. Tone: warm, plain-spoken, lightly witty. // no "elevated" or "passionate" — those are tired. Format: — Hero headline (max 8 words) — Sub-headline (one sentence) — 3 short paragraphs: beans, brunch, the room — One short call to action Don't use: "passionate", "artisanal", "journey".
