Part Two

Wireframes &
prototypes.

The boring slide that saves your weekend. Plan the structure first, style it later, build it last — in roughly that order.

If you've ever rebuilt a homepage three times because the client “changed their mind”, this section is for you.
Definition

A wireframe is the
blueprint, not the building.

It's a low-fidelity visual plan that answers what goes where — not what colour it is, what font it uses, or what the hero image looks like.

Grey boxes for images. Placeholder text. No styling. The point is structure, hierarchy, and flow — agreed before the expensive work starts.

The Case For Planning

Cheap now beats expensive later.

01

Catch the gaps

You'll find missing pages, missing copy, and missing flows — while it costs minutes to fix, not days.

02

Align with the client

Show structure before style. Disagreements about “where the contact form lives” are easier to settle on a sketch than on built code.

03

Protect your time

The number of times a wireframe has saved me from rebuilding a whole page from scratch — I've stopped counting.

Rule of thumb: 10 minutes planning saves an hour building. An hour planning can save a weekend rebuilding.

A Repeatable Process

Same order. Every project.

It works whether the site has 1 page or 50. The earlier steps cost almost nothing — skip them and you'll pay for it later.

  • Discover — who's the audience, what's the goal, what does "done" look like?
  • Sitemap — what pages exist and how do they connect?
  • User flows — what's the path through a task (e.g. "buy a thing")?
  • Wireframes — structure per page, no style.
  • Content — real words, not lorem. (Or as close as you can get.)
  • Mockups — apply the visual design.
  • Prototype — click through to test the flows.
  • Build — code it.
  • Test & ship — watch someone actually use it.

Part Three

AI tools — what to use,
what to watch out for.

Some of these platforms are extraordinary. Some of them have track records you should know about before you paste your client's data into them.

The goal isn't to ban tools. It's to choose them with your eyes open.
Things You Probably Didn't Know

About ChatGPT & Copilot.

April 2023

Samsung banned ChatGPT company-wide after engineers leaked confidential source code into it three times in 20 days. Once the data is in OpenAI's servers, you can't pull it back.

December 2024

Italy's data protection authority fined OpenAI €15 million — the first GDPR fine ever issued against a generative AI company — for training ChatGPT on personal data without proper legal basis. (Overturned on appeal March 2026, but the case still shaped EU law.)

GitHub Copilot

Sued in a class action (Doe v. GitHub) for training on open-source code without honouring the licenses. Copilot has been caught suggesting code that's near-verbatim copies of training data — including comments and variable names.

Default settings

On free / personal tiers, your conversations train the model unless you turn it off. That includes anything you paste in. Read the terms before you paste a client brief.

Anthropic

Claude.

Made by Anthropic, founded by ex-OpenAI safety researchers. Built with "Constitutional AI" — safety principles baked into the training, not bolted on.

It's also what I used to build this slideshow you're looking at right now.

Strong at

  • Long-form writing & editing — tone, voice, polish
  • Reading & reasoning over big documents (200k token context ≈ 150k words)
  • Code review, refactoring, debugging
  • Live HTML/code artifacts in the chat (like this one)
  • Projects with persistent context & reusable Skills
  • Computer Use — driving a browser to actually do tasks
claude.ai  ·  docs.claude.com
Google DeepMind

Gemini.

Google's family of models — natively multimodal (text, image, audio, video all at once) with a context window that goes well past 1 million tokens.

Plays well with the Workspace tools you already use — Docs, Sheets, Gmail, Drive.

Strong at

  • Deep Research — multi-source web research with citations
  • NotebookLM — turn your own PDFs & notes into a podcast-style audio overview
  • Native image understanding & generation (Imagen)
  • Native video generation (Veo)
  • Massive context — feed it whole textbooks, whole codebases
  • Built right into Google Workspace if your work lives there
gemini.google.com  ·  notebooklm.google

Prompt Craft

Better prompts,
better results.

You don't need to memorise a framework. You need to give it what a thoughtful human would need: role, context, examples, format.

  • Set the role. "You are a senior front-end developer reviewing junior code."
  • Give context. Audience, goal, brand voice, constraints.
  • Show examples. One good example > ten sentences of description.
  • Specify the format. Length, structure, what NOT to include.
  • Iterate. First answer is a draft. Tell it what to fix.
Bad prompt write me a homepage for a coffee shop

Better prompt You are a copywriter for small Perth hospitality businesses.

Write a homepage for Pulse Coffee, a Mt Lawley specialty cafe.

Audience: locals, 25–40, who want good coffee without pretension.

Tone: warm, plain-spoken, lightly witty. No "elevated" or "passionate" — those are tired.

Format:
— Hero headline (max 8 words)
— Sub-headline (one sentence)
— 3 short paragraphs about beans, brunch, & the room
— One short call to action

Don't: use the words "passionate", "artisanal", or "journey".

Your Job This Week

Plan your
original site.

Due Monday 25 May. Spend the rest of today setting yourself up properly — the build will be much smoother for it.

Do today

  • Pick what the site is & who it's for
  • Sketch a sitemap — what pages, in what order
  • Wireframe the homepage at minimum (Excalidraw / Figma / paper)
  • Gather any content you've got — copy, images, brand stuff
  • Note the questions you'll need answered by Friday

Don't yet

  • Don't open a code editor
  • Don't pick fonts or colours
  • Don't ask Claude or Gemini to "build it for you"
  • Don't lorem-ipsum your way through it — plan the real content
